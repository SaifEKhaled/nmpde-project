Methodology I: Explicit Leapfrog

1. Mathematical Discretization

The standard 2D scalar wave equation is governed by the continuous partial differential equation:

$$ \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} - c^2 \Delta u = f(\mathbf{x}, t) \quad \text{in } \Omega \times (0, T] $$

By applying the Galerkin Finite Element Method (FEM) using $Q_4$ spectral shape functions, we map the continuous domain into a semi-discrete system of ordinary differential equations. This yields the global matrix formulation:

$$ M \mathbf{\ddot{u}}(t) + K \mathbf{u}(t) = \mathbf{F}(t) $$

where $M$ is the global mass matrix and $K$ is the stiffness matrix (Laplacian operator). To integrate this system through time without the prohibitive computational overhead of implicit solvers, we selected the Explicit Leapfrog (Störmer-Verlet) method. Approximating the second derivative via a central difference scheme yields our core C++ update rule:

$$ \mathbf{u}^{n+1} = 2\mathbf{u}^n - \mathbf{u}^{n-1} - \Delta t^2 M^{-1}K \mathbf{u}^n $$

2. Architectural Approach: Why Leapfrog?

In High-Performance Computing, numerical dissipation—where a solver artificially drains energy from the system to remain stable—is a critical flaw for acoustic and electromagnetic wave propagation. We chose Leapfrog specifically because it is a Geometric Numerical Integrator.

The Symplectic Mandate: The physical wave equation is a Hamiltonian system, meaning its total energy (Kinetic + Potential) must remain constant. The Leapfrog method is symplectic; it preserves the differential 2-form $dp \wedge dq$ of the phase space geometry. While its local truncation error is only $\mathcal{O}(\Delta t^2)$, its structural preservation guarantees that the numerical energy will oscillate around the true energy without ever drifting to infinity or collapsing to zero over infinite time horizons.

3. Empirical Validation Suite

To rigorously prove the stability and accuracy of the C++ implementation, the engine was subjected to four distinct stress tests.

Test I: Hamiltonian Conservation

Analysis: The simulation was executed for $T=10.0s$ at a stable Courant number ($CFL = 0.5$). As expected of a 2nd-order symplectic method, the relative energy $E_t/E_0$ exhibits jagged, high-frequency oscillations. Crucially, however, the phase envelope is strictly bounded, proving zero numerical dissipation.

Test II: Phase Space Topology

Analysis: To visualize the multidimensional phase space, we utilized Takens' Delay Embedding Theorem, plotting $E(t)$ against $E(t + 5\Delta t)$. The resulting "chaotic attractor" is perfectly enclosed. If the method were non-symplectic, this topology would spiral outward or collapse entirely.

Test III: Temporal Convergence

Analysis: By systematically refining the time step, we mapped the $L_2$ error norm. The log-log linear regression yields an empirical slope of exactly 2.00, achieving perfect mathematical alignment with the theoretical $\mathcal{O}(\Delta t^2)$ convergence rate of the Störmer-Verlet scheme.

Test IV: The CFL Limit

Analysis: The method's primary weakness is its conditional stability. By pushing the parameter to $CFL = 1.5$, the wave propagation speed exceeded the maximum stable grid velocity $c \frac{\Delta t}{\Delta x} > 1$. The amplification matrix eigenvalues exceeded unity, resulting in immediate, exponential numerical explosion ($10^{91}$ Joules).
